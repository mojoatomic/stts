# State Topology and Trajectory Storage:
## A Geometric Framework for Monitoring Complex Dynamic Systems

*Doug Fennell · Preprint · 2026 · arXiv:cs.LG, eess.SY, cs.DB*

---

## Abstract

Contemporary monitoring of complex dynamic systems relies on a storage and query model inherited from relational database theory: system state is represented as discrete snapshots, and alerts are generated by threshold violation. This model is structurally mismatched to systems in which state evolves continuously, failure is a trajectory, and the operationally relevant question is not "what is the current state" but "what does the current trajectory resemble."

We propose State Topology and Trajectory Storage (STTS), a framework in which systems are represented as continuous trajectories through an n-dimensional embedding space, stored in a vector-queryable index, and monitored by geometric similarity search against a corpus of historical trajectories with known outcomes. The primary monitoring query is a nearest-neighbor search: how similar is the current trajectory to trajectories that preceded known failure states. Under stated conditions, this query fires before any individual parameter threshold is crossed, recovering an intervention window that threshold monitoring cannot see.

We identify three applicability conditions under which the framework provides meaningful value, and argue that eight domains satisfy them: aerospace, launch systems, marine transport, clinical medicine, power grid stability, financial systems, epidemiology, and structural integrity. Cross-validated empirical results on the NASA C-MAPSS turbofan benchmark — four sub-datasets spanning single and multi-condition operation — yield F1 scores of 0.88–0.97, exceeding the closest domain-specific prior art on three of four held-out evaluations. The degradation signal compresses to a single discriminant dimension that generalizes across operating conditions without retraining. Validation on NASA battery degradation data confirms the framework's geometric structure in a third physical domain — electrochemical capacity fade — with identical pipeline architecture: V1 separation of 320.9x (p < 10⁻⁸³) and detection of all 10 run-to-failure batteries before end-of-life. Validation on near-Earth asteroid close approach trajectories — orbital elements computed by JPL Horizons from DE441 numerical integration — extends the framework to a fourth physical domain: 973 confirmed Earth close approaches, V1 = 3.8x (p ≈ 0), V2 ρ = 0.631, F1 = 1.000 [95% CI: 0.998–1.000] on 795 held-out test objects. With 1,825-day trajectory histories, mean detection lead reaches 1,693 days (4.6 years) before close approach; 57.6% of objects are detected within 90 days of any point in their tracked history, and no object requires more than 665 days of history to trigger detection. The distribution is right-truncated at the 1,825-day window — the signal precedes the available data. Applied out-of-sample to asteroid 99942 Apophis, the framework produces a triage signal from 45 days of observational arc, 24.4 years before the 2029 flyby, using a corpus that contained no Apophis observations. Across four physically distinct domains — turbofan engines, bearings, batteries, and orbital mechanics — the framework's geometric structure (V1) holds universally regardless of corpus size, while detection performance (V2, F1) tracks corpus sufficiency monotonically, providing empirical evidence that the stated applicability condition P1 is a binding constraint. We further present illustrative analyses of two historical events — STS-107 Columbia and STS-51-L Challenger — tracing precursor trajectory signatures in published forensic records prior to threshold violation.

## 1. Introduction

On the morning of February 1, 2003, Space Shuttle Columbia re-entered Earth's atmosphere carrying seven crew members and a thermal protection system compromised sixteen days earlier by a foam strike during ascent. Every sensor aboard the vehicle was operating. Every reading was being recorded. The monitoring systems were functioning exactly as designed. By the time any parameter crossed a threshold that would have triggered an automated alert, the vehicle had already begun to disintegrate.

This paper argues that Columbia's monitoring systems failed not because they malfunctioned, but because they were built on a model whose limitations are rarely examined as a class. The model stores snapshots of system state and generates alerts when individual parameters cross predefined boundaries. It is precise, auditable, and fast. It is also structurally incapable of seeing what killed Columbia: not a threshold crossing, but a trajectory approaching a failure basin that the monitoring system had no language to describe.

The limitation is not peculiar to aerospace. The same structural issue allowed a sepsis spiral to resemble stable vital signs until the patient was already in crisis. The same issue left grid operators watching individual node states while the 2003 Northeast blackout propagated along a cascade trajectory no single sensor could see. In each case, the monitoring model was correctly answering a question about instantaneous state while failing to ask a question about trajectory.

The question that threshold monitoring cannot ask is: what does this trajectory resemble, and what came next in similar historical cases?

We propose a framework — State Topology and Trajectory Storage (STTS) — built on a different primitive. In STTS, every system is represented not as a point in state space but as a continuous trajectory through it. That trajectory is embedded in a vector space designed to preserve geometric similarity between trajectories. Monitoring is performed by nearest-neighbor search: how similar is the current trajectory to trajectories that preceded known failure states? The answer to that question is available before any individual parameter crosses a limit.

The mathematical tools required are not novel. Metric spaces, manifold embedding, and approximate nearest-neighbor search are mature fields with production-ready infrastructure. The contribution is applying them as a storage and query primitive for operational monitoring — not as a performance optimization over the relational model, but as a complementary primitive for a specific and consequential query class that relational storage does not natively support.

### 1.1 Scope and applicability

STTS is the appropriate primitive for systems in which state evolves continuously with causal connection between successive states, failure modes recur in geometrically similar patterns across instances, and the cost of late detection exceeds the cost of similarity-based monitoring infrastructure. We formalize these as three applicability conditions in Section 3 and identify eight domains in which they are satisfied: aerospace, launch systems, marine transport, clinical medicine, power grid stability, financial systems, epidemiology, and structural integrity.

The central claim is not that STTS is a better maintenance algorithm for a specific domain. It is that there exists a large and consequential class of complex dynamic systems for which threshold-based monitoring is a structurally limited choice, and that a geometric, trajectory-based alternative is implementable with infrastructure available today.

### 1.2 Vantage point

The argument that follows is made from within safety-critical systems engineering. The threshold model was built by serious engineers working under genuine constraints — the need for deterministic, auditable, real-time decisions in systems where the cost of a false alarm could itself be catastrophic. That model deserves respect. This paper does not dismiss it. It argues that the model has a structural boundary and that the cost of that boundary is no longer acceptable in domains where early detection determines outcomes.

The specific case of Columbia is used throughout not to assign blame — the Columbia Accident Investigation Board conducted that analysis with appropriate rigor — but because it is the clearest available demonstration of a monitoring system with complete, functioning sensors that nonetheless could not see the relevant signal. The trajectory was there. The data existed. The model had no mechanism to ask the relevant question.

### 1.3 Relationship to existing work

The Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) field has produced genuine improvements in predictive maintenance across several domains.[^1] Importantly, trajectory-aware methods already exist within PHM. Wang et al.'s Trajectory Similarity Based Prediction (TSBP) compares current degradation trajectories against a library of historical run-to-failure trajectories.[^1b] Dynamic Time Warping is widely used for trajectory similarity comparison.[^6b] Zhang et al. reconstruct phase-space trajectories and measure similarity using normalized cross-correlation.[^1c] Jules et al. use siamese networks with triplet loss to learn multi-dimensional degradation-aware embeddings.[^1d] These are real trajectory methods producing real results.

STTS does not claim methodological novelty over this body of work. It claims architectural novelty. The contribution is threefold.

First, **cross-domain unification**. Existing trajectory methods are developed and validated within single domains. STTS proposes that the same mathematical primitive (F → W → M) applies across aerospace, clinical medicine, power grids, and finance. A framework in which only the domain instantiation changes across eight domains is making a different kind of claim than a domain-specific prediction tool.

Second, **trajectory embedding as production storage primitive**. Existing PHM methods compute trajectory similarity as an analytical layer on top of relational or time-series storage. STTS proposes embedding trajectories at ingest time into a vector-queryable index, making nearest-neighbor similarity the native operational query — continuous, real-time, and available without per-query reconstruction.

Third, **the reframe from prediction to resemblance**. PHM asks "what is the remaining useful life?" — a point prediction. STTS asks "what does this trajectory resemble, and what came next?" — a similarity query. The output is interpretable by construction: the system returns the most similar historical trajectories with their actual outcomes, which an operator can inspect and evaluate.

The relationship to Codd's relational model clarifies the nature of the contribution. Codd's 1970 insight was that the navigational model made certain query classes — joins, aggregations, ad hoc filtering — structurally intractable regardless of implementation quality.[^2] He proposed a storage primitive that made those queries native. STTS makes an analogous but narrower observation: continuous real-time trajectory similarity monitoring is not natively supported by relational or time-series storage regardless of how sophisticated the algorithms layered on top become. Relational storage remains appropriate for structured queries over operational data — maintenance records, configuration states, scheduled events. STTS proposes a complementary primitive for geometric similarity search over trajectory embeddings.

### 1.4 Paper organization

Section 2 develops the epistemological argument for why the relational model is structurally limited for trajectory-based monitoring. Section 3 presents the mathematical framework: formal definitions, the monitoring query, out-of-distribution detection, and the intervention window proposition. Section 4 develops the embedding function φ in depth. Section 5 instantiates the framework across eight domains. Section 9 discusses implications and concludes.

## 2. The Epistemological Argument

Every monitoring system embodies a theory of what matters. The theory embedded in current practice is this: what matters is the current value of observable parameters, compared against the range of values those parameters are permitted to take. When a value leaves its permitted range, an alert is generated. When it returns, the alert clears.

This theory has considerable virtues. It is deterministic. It is auditable — any alert can be traced to a specific parameter and a specific threshold crossing. It is fast — comparison against a scalar bound requires essentially no computation. These are not trivial virtues in systems where reliability is measured in lives.

The theory also has a structural limitation that no implementation can correct.

### 2.1 The structural lateness of threshold monitoring

Consider what it means to detect a threshold crossing. A parameter s_i(t) is monitored against a bound θ_i. Detection occurs at time t* when:

```
s_i(t*) ≥ θ_i
```

By definition, t* is the moment of crossing — the moment the boundary is reached. Detection cannot precede crossing. This is not a limitation of sensor technology, sampling rate, or computational speed. It is a logical property of the model. The boundary is placed at the edge of the acceptable region, which means the system has already traversed the entire acceptable region before detection occurs.

Engineers know this. The standard response is to place thresholds conservatively — inside the boundary of the truly dangerous region, so that alert-to-danger time provides an intervention window. This is sound practice within the model. It does not change the model's fundamental property: detection is reactive, defined by boundary crossing, and structurally unable to characterize approach.

The threshold model cannot distinguish a system at a parameter value from a system that has been drifting toward that value for six hours. Both produce identical readings at the moment of inspection. Only one is dangerous.

This distinction — between a state and a trajectory arriving at that state — is precisely what threshold monitoring discards. The current value of s(t) carries no information about the path by which the system arrived there. Two systems at identical current states, one drifting steadily toward a failure region and one oscillating stably near its operating point, are indistinguishable by any threshold comparison. Their trajectories are different. Their risk profiles are different. The monitoring model encodes neither.

### 2.2 What is discarded

The information discarded by the relational model is precisely the information that distinguishes imminent failure from normal operation in the regime where intervention is still possible.

Four categories of information are systematically absent from point-in-time monitoring.

**Rate of change.** The derivative of s(t) with respect to time — is the temperature rising or falling? — carries more predictive information than the absolute value of s(t) in many failure modes. A temperature of 340K and rising is a different condition from a temperature of 340K and stable. Threshold monitoring cannot encode this distinction without adding derivative channels — which are threshold channels, not trajectory channels.

**Cross-parameter covariance.** The joint evolution of multiple parameters over time encodes failure-mode information that individual parameters do not. Sepsis does not manifest as one vital sign crossing a threshold. It manifests as a characteristic pattern of joint physiological drift — heart rate and respiratory rate rising together while blood pressure falls, lactate rising as tissue perfusion degrades — that is not captured by any threshold on any individual parameter.

**Temporal pattern.** Intermittent threshold crossings that resolve and recur carry different risk implications than a single sustained crossing. A bearing that vibrates excessively three times per week at the same operating load is on a degradation trajectory that a single crossing event does not convey.

**Historical resemblance.** The most important information is also the most absent: does the current system state — not the current parameter values, but the current trajectory — resemble trajectories that preceded failure in historical records? This is the question that experienced engineers ask intuitively. It is the question the monitoring model has no architecture to answer.

### 2.3 Why this is architectural, not implementational

A natural response is that sophisticated implementations can recover this information: add derivative channels, compute rolling correlations, build ML models that learn failure precursors from historical data. These are legitimate approaches and they produce genuine improvements.

But they are algorithmic additions to a model that remains relational at its storage primitive. The derivative of s(t) is computed and stored as a new parameter — a point in extended state space. A rolling correlation is computed and compared against a threshold — a threshold on a derived quantity. An ML model trained on historical trajectories is evaluated on the current feature vector — a point comparison, not a trajectory query.

None of these recover the architectural property that STTS provides: a nearest-neighbor query that asks, on every monitoring cycle, whether the current trajectory resembles the trajectories in a labeled historical corpus. That query requires trajectory embeddings stored in a vector-queryable index. It is not achievable by adding features to a relational model regardless of the sophistication of the features.

### 2.4 Relationship to existing PHM trajectory methods

The PHM field has developed trajectory-aware methods that address some of these limitations.[^1b,1c,1d,6b,6c,6d] These methods demonstrate that trajectory-based approaches are viable and that they outperform threshold-based baselines on benchmark datasets. The gap between these methods and STTS is architectural, as described in §1.3: existing methods apply trajectory comparison as an analytical layer on demand, not as a continuously-served production storage primitive. The practical consequence is that existing methods require per-query trajectory reconstruction, cannot serve continuous real-time monitoring at sensor sampling rates, and are not designed to grow automatically as new labeled trajectories arrive.

## 3. Mathematical Framework

We develop the formal framework for STTS. Definitions 1–7 establish the objects of the theory. Proposition 1 states the central operational property. The applicability conditions bound the domain of valid application.

### 3.1 State and trajectory

**Definition 1 (State vector).** *The state of a system S at time t is a vector s(t) ∈ ℝⁿ, where n is the number of observable parameters.*

**Definition 2 (State trajectory).** *The trajectory of system S over interval [t₀, t₁] is:*

```
𝒯 = { s(t) : t ∈ [t₀, t₁] }  ⊂  ℝⁿ
```

*𝒯 is a continuous curve in state space when state evolution satisfies causal continuity (Condition C1).*

### 3.2 Embedding function

**Definition 3 (Embedding function).** *An embedding function φ : 𝒯 → ℝᵈ maps a trajectory to a fixed-dimensional vector, satisfying similarity preservation:*

```
sim(𝒯₁, 𝒯₂) ≈ sim(φ(𝒯₁), φ(𝒯₂))
```

*where sim is a domain-appropriate similarity measure. The mapping is from trajectories — curves in state space — not from individual state vectors.*

The embedding φ is composed of three stages: φ = M(W · F(𝒯)). F extracts a feature vector from the trajectory window. W applies a causal weighting matrix that amplifies features with known causal relationships to failure. M projects the weighted feature vector to the monitoring subspace. Section 4 develops each stage in detail.

### 3.3 Failure basin and corpus

**Definition 4 (Failure basin).** *The failure basin ℬ_f is the set of trajectory embeddings that precede failure within a consequence window Δt:*

```
ℬ_f = { φ(𝒯) : failure event occurs within [t₁, t₁ + Δt] }
```

*ℬ_f is constructed from labeled historical trajectories. It is not specified analytically — it is empirically populated from the corpus.*

**Definition 5 (Historical corpus).** *The corpus 𝒞 is a labeled collection of trajectory embeddings with associated outcomes and metadata:*

```
𝒞 = { (φ(𝒯ᵢ), oᵢ, mᵢ) : i = 1, ..., N }
```

*where oᵢ ∈ 𝒪 is the outcome label and mᵢ is metadata (timestamp, system identifier, operating context).*

### 3.4 The monitoring query

**Definition 6 (Monitoring query).** *At each monitoring cycle, the system computes the distance from the current trajectory embedding to the failure basin:*

```
d(φ(𝒯_now), ℬ_f) = min_{φ(𝒯) ∈ ℬ_f}  ‖ φ(𝒯_now) − φ(𝒯) ‖
```

*The system issues an alert when this distance falls below a calibrated threshold ε:*

```
Alert  ⟺  d(φ(𝒯_now), ℬ_f)  <  ε
```

In practice, d is computed as the mean distance to the K nearest neighbors in ℬ_f rather than the global minimum, to reduce sensitivity to outlier embeddings in the corpus.

### 3.5 Out-of-distribution detection

**Definition 7 (OOD detection).** *Let 𝒦(𝒞) denote the convex hull of all corpus embeddings. The OOD signal δ_OOD(φ(𝒯_now)) is the distance from the current embedding to 𝒦(𝒞):*

```
δ_OOD = dist(φ(𝒯_now), 𝒦(𝒞))
```

*When δ_OOD > 0, the current trajectory is outside the operational envelope of the corpus. No similarity-based prediction is reliable in this region.*

The four-state monitoring output derived from the primary and OOD queries is:

- **NOMINAL**: d(φ(𝒯_now), ℬ_f) > ε_watch and δ_OOD ≈ 0 — confirmed safe, corpus well-covered
- **WATCH**: ε > d > ε_watch and δ_OOD ≈ 0 — intermediate distance, monitoring closely
- **FAILURE_APPROACH**: d < ε — alert, intervention indicated
- **TERRA_INCOGNITA**: δ_OOD > 0 — trajectory outside corpus coverage, expert review required

The TERRA_INCOGNITA state is distinct from FAILURE_APPROACH: it does not indicate that failure is approaching, but that the corpus has no data in the current region. This is the appropriate response to genuinely novel configurations, such as the STS-51-L launch temperature in the Challenger case (§6.6.2).

### 3.6 Verification requirements

The embedding φ must satisfy three empirically verifiable conditions before deployment.

**V1 — Basin separation.** The median distance from nominal trajectory embeddings to ℬ_f must exceed the median distance from precursor trajectory embeddings to ℬ_f by a factor exceeding 2x (domain-dependent). Verified by Mann-Whitney U test on the two distance distributions.

**V2 — Monotonic approach.** For trajectories known to end in failure, the distance d(φ(𝒯(t)), ℬ_f) must decrease monotonically as failure approaches. Verified by Spearman rank correlation between time-to-failure and basin distance across training trajectories.

**V3 — Causal traceability.** The features driving proximity to ℬ_f must be traceable to established causal mechanisms for the failure mode in question, verified by feature ablation analysis and domain expert review.

### 3.6c False alarm characterization

STTS has a structural argument for lower false alarm rates than point-in-time threshold systems: a trajectory-level similarity query requires sustained geometric resemblance to failure precursors over a time window, not a transient parameter excursion at a single moment. A brief vital sign spike embedded in a nominal six-hour trajectory will not produce high similarity to failure-precursor trajectories in ℬ_f.

However, this structural argument is not a proof. The actual false alarm rate depends on the quality of φ, the density and labeling accuracy of the corpus, the choice of ε, and domain-specific characteristics of nominal variability. Empirical false alarm characterization — false positive rate as a function of ε, stratified by operational context — is a required component of any domain deployment and must be reported alongside detection sensitivity.

### 3.7 Proposition 1 — The intervention window

**Proposition 1 (Intervention window).** *Let S be a system with state trajectory 𝒯 approaching a failure event at time t_f. Let t_threshold be the time at which the first parameter crosses its threshold bound. Let t_STTS be the time at which d(φ(𝒯_now), ℬ_f) first drops below ε. Under conditions (P1)–(P3):*

```
t_STTS  ≤  t_threshold  ≤  t_f
```

*The interval [t_STTS, t_threshold] is the intervention window recovered by STTS.*

**Conditions:**

**(P1) Corpus sufficiency.** ℬ_f is populated with labeled trajectories from prior failure events of the relevant type within consequence window Δt.

**(P2) Embedding fidelity.** φ satisfies similarity preservation with sufficient precision that trajectories approaching failure along novel paths are mapped near ℬ_f before their threshold crossings. Verified empirically by V1 and V2.

**(P3) Threshold conservatism.** Threshold bounds θᵢ are set at the edge of the acceptable operating region — standard engineering practice.

This is stated as a proposition rather than a theorem because its validity depends on empirically verifiable conditions, not logical consequence alone. A formal theorem would require: (a) sample complexity bounds on ℬ_f estimation from finite corpus, (b) error bounds on φ's similarity preservation as a function of corpus size and embedding dimension d, and (c) a formal model of the relationship between trajectory approach dynamics and threshold crossing times. These are tractable problems in statistical learning theory and constitute important directions for future theoretical work. Section 6 provides empirical evidence that Proposition 1 holds under conditions (P1)–(P3) on the evaluated datasets.

### 3.8 Applicability conditions

**Applicability conditions.** *STTS provides meaningful monitoring value for system S when the following three conditions are satisfied:*

**C1 — Causal continuity.** *State evolution is causally connected: s(t+δ) is a continuous function of s(t) and the system's dynamics. The trajectory is a causally coherent curve through state space, not a sequence of independent observations.*

**C2 — Recurrent geometry.** *Failure modes recur in geometrically similar patterns across instances. Distinct failure events of the same type produce trajectory embeddings that cluster in ℬ_f. Prior trajectories are geometrically informative about future trajectories of the same type.*

**C3 — Cost asymmetry.** *The expected cost of late detection by threshold monitoring exceeds the cost of deploying and maintaining a trajectory similarity monitoring system.*

These conditions are stated as engineering conditions, not as a biconditional theorem — the claim is that they are jointly sufficient for STTS to provide meaningful value, not that their absence is formally provable to preclude it.

C1 ensures that the trajectory is a meaningful object — it has causal structure rather than being an arbitrary sequence of points. Systems that violate C1, such as memoryless stochastic systems, do not have trajectories in the relevant sense.

C2 ensures that the corpus is useful. Without recurrent geometry, prior trajectories carry no information about future ones, and the nearest-neighbor query has no predictive value. C2 is satisfied whenever the physical mechanisms of failure are stable.

C3 acknowledges that STTS has infrastructure costs — embedding pipeline, vector index, corpus maintenance — and that these are only justified when the value of the recovered intervention window exceeds them.

## 4. The Embedding Function φ

The embedding function φ = M(W · F(𝒯)) is the operational core of the framework. This section develops each stage.

### 4.1 Stage F — Feature extraction

F extracts a fixed-dimensional feature vector from a sliding trajectory window. Four feature classes capture the information that threshold monitoring discards.

**Time-domain summaries** (mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum per sensor channel) encode the distribution of parameter values over the window. These are the features closest to threshold monitoring — they summarize where the parameters have been.

**Rate features** (first and second derivative statistics per channel) encode the velocity and acceleration of parameter drift. These are the simplest form of trajectory information absent from point-in-time monitoring.

**Frequency-domain features** (FFT spectral energy per channel at selected frequencies) encode periodic and quasi-periodic patterns characteristic of specific failure modes. Bearing defect signatures, blade passing frequencies, and combustion instabilities all manifest as spectral anomalies before time-domain degradation is measurable.

**Cross-parameter covariance structure** (pairwise correlations and eigenvalues of the covariance matrix) encodes the joint evolution of the state vector. This is the feature class most absent from threshold monitoring — it captures how parameters move together, which encodes failure-mode information that individual parameter trajectories do not.

**Implementation note.** Feature standardization must occur before causal weighting W, not after. Standardizing after W erases the differential amplification W encodes — all features return to unit variance regardless of their assigned weight.

### 4.2 Stage W — Causal weighting

W is a diagonal weighting matrix applied to the feature vector produced by F. Its purpose is to amplify features with known causal relationships to the failure mode being monitored and suppress features that encode operational variation unrelated to failure.

For well-understood physical systems, W encodes domain knowledge: rate-of-change features and cross-parameter covariance are up-weighted; absolute level features that respond primarily to operating condition rather than degradation state are down-weighted. On simulated datasets lacking the physics of actual operational variation (such as C-MAPSS), uniform weights are appropriate because no domain knowledge is available to differentiate causal from non-causal features.

### 4.3 Stage M — Metric projection

M projects the causally-weighted feature vector to a lower-dimensional monitoring subspace. For physical degradation monitoring, we use Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) with RUL-bucketed class labels. LDA learns a projection that maximally separates degradation states while minimizing within-class variance — it suppresses variation that does not discriminate between health states, which includes operating-condition variation in multi-regime datasets.

M is the appropriate stage for learning because it operates on causally-structured features that F and W have already organized. Learning directly on raw sensor data risks encoding operational artifacts; learning on causally-structured features encodes degradation geometry.

### 4.4 Verification protocol

Before deployment, the embedding φ must pass the three verification conditions. V1 is verified by Mann-Whitney U test on the distance distributions of nominal and precursor trajectory embeddings in the training corpus. V2 is verified by Spearman rank correlation between time-to-failure and basin distance. V3 is verified by feature ablation analysis: systematically removing feature classes and observing the change in V1 separation, then confirming that the driving features are causally connected to the known failure mechanism.

A deployment in which V1 and V2 pass but V3 cannot be satisfied should be treated with caution: a statistically valid but causally uninterpretable embedding may reflect data artifacts rather than genuine failure-precursor geometry.

## 5. Domain Instantiations

The three applicability conditions (C1, C2, C3) and the three-stage embedding pipeline (F → W → M) apply across all eight domains. The instantiation specifies the state vector, the feature extraction logic, the causal weighting structure, and the consequence window Δt appropriate to each domain. Here we provide the instantiation for each domain sufficient for implementation.

The fact that the same three-stage structure describes the instantiation in all eight cases is itself evidence for the framework's generality claim: only the domain-specific content of F and W changes. M applies the same projection logic regardless of domain.

### 5.1 Aerospace — turbofan engine degradation

The turbofan case is the most empirically developed, with quantitative results reported in Section 6. The state vector s(t) includes thermocouple readings at multiple compressor and turbine stages, total pressures and temperatures at inlet and exhaust, fan and core shaft speeds, fuel flow rate, and bypass ratio. The primary failure modes relevant to the C-MAPSS benchmark are High Pressure Compressor (HPC) degradation and fan degradation.

F extracts time-domain summaries, rate features, FFT features at low-frequency bands, and cross-sensor covariance structure from a 30-cycle sliding window, yielding a 264-dimensional feature vector per window. W is set to uniform weights on the C-MAPSS simulation data (which lacks the causal chain physics that would inform differential weighting). M is a 1-component LDA projection fitted on training data with RUL-bucketed class labels.

The consequence window Δt = 25 cycles corresponds to the WARNING_RUL threshold. C1 is satisfied by the continuous physical degradation process. C2 is satisfied by the recurrent HPC and fan failure physics. C3 is satisfied by the operational cost of unscheduled engine maintenance.

### 5.2 Launch systems

The launch vehicle case extends the aerospace instantiation to a system with no operational run-to-failure data — each launch is unique. The state vector includes propellant pressures, temperatures, and flow rates across all propulsion stages; turbopump shaft speeds and bearing temperatures; thrust vector control actuator positions and response times; and structural loads at key interfaces.

W down-weights absolute pressure and temperature readings (which vary legitimately with flight phase and altitude) and up-weights rate-of-change features and inter-system covariance — the pattern of correlated pressure transients that distinguishes normal acoustic coupling from combustion instability onset. The consequence window Δt is measured in seconds to tens of seconds.

The corpus for launch systems is thin by C-MAPSS standards — few fleets have hundreds of run-to-failure events in their operational history. This makes P1 the binding constraint. The TERRA_INCOGNITA signal (Definition 7) is therefore more frequently the correct monitoring output than FAILURE_APPROACH in this domain: novel failure modes in a sparse corpus should return "outside operational envelope" rather than "approaching known failure geometry."

### 5.3 Marine transport

Marine degradation monitoring spans machinery health (main engine, gearbox, propeller shaft bearings, auxiliary systems) and structural integrity (hull stress, fatigue at frame connections, weld quality degradation). The state vector for machinery monitoring includes vibration signatures at key bearing locations, lube oil temperature and contamination indicators, exhaust gas temperatures and turbocharger speeds, and shaft torsional load.

The multi-regime challenge is significant in the marine domain: a vessel operating in heavy weather loads its systems differently from calm-water transit. W must down-weight features that respond primarily to sea state and up-weight features that respond to mechanical degradation independent of loading. The consequence window is days to weeks for slow-progressing bearing degradation and hours for acute machinery faults.

### 5.4 Clinical medicine — sepsis

The clinical instantiation is fully specified as a validation protocol in Section 6.7. The state vector s(t) includes heart rate, mean arterial pressure, respiratory rate, temperature, oxygen saturation, and lactate concentration. The cross-parameter covariance structure is critical: sepsis manifests as a characteristic joint distributional trajectory that is not captured by any individual parameter threshold.

W weights rate-of-change features and the cross-vital covariance structure heavily, consistent with established sepsis physiology: the physiological cascade is characterized by correlated changes across multiple vital parameters rather than isolated parameter drift. The consequence window Δt is 6 hours, consistent with the PhysioNet 2019 challenge definition of early detection.

### 5.5 Power grid stability

Grid stability monitoring is characterized by very short consequence windows (seconds to minutes for cascade failure), very high-dimensional state vectors (thousands of nodes in a large grid), and a failure mode — cascade propagation along overloaded transmission paths — that is fundamentally topological. The state vector includes real and reactive power flows on monitored transmission lines, nodal voltage magnitudes and angles, frequency at monitored buses, and protective relay status.

The cross-parameter covariance structure is particularly important here: the pattern of which lines are simultaneously near their thermal limits encodes topological vulnerability that individual line loadings do not. W up-weights inter-line correlation features and the rate of change of voltage angles.

### 5.6 Financial systems

Financial systemic risk monitoring faces a challenge absent from physical systems: the failure basin ℬ_f cannot be constructed from laboratory conditions — it must be built from the limited historical record of actual crises. The 2008 financial crisis, the 1998 LTCM episode, and the 1987 Black Monday episode provide the primary labeled precursor trajectories for a crisis corpus.

The state vector includes cross-asset correlations (equity, fixed income, credit default swaps, foreign exchange), liquidity indicators (bid-ask spreads, repo market volumes), volatility term structure, and leverage ratios across major counterparties. W up-weights the rate of change of cross-asset correlations — the signature of correlation breakdown or sudden correlation convergence that characterizes systemic stress.

C2 is the weakest condition in financial systems: the institutional structures and instrument types of each crisis episode are somewhat novel, which limits the geometric similarity of precursor trajectories across crises. The TERRA_INCOGNITA signal will be triggered frequently in this domain — a feature, not a limitation, given the genuine novelty of each financial crisis.

### 5.7 Epidemiology

The epidemiological instantiation monitors the early spread of infectious disease through a population. The state vector s(t) includes case count time series disaggregated by geography, age cohort, and severity; mobility data where available; genomic sequence cluster counts; and hospitalization utilization rates.

The consequence window is weeks to months — appropriate for surveillance systems intended to detect exponential growth before it becomes unmanageable. The corpus ℬ_f is constructed from prior outbreak trajectories: the early spread dynamics of SARS-CoV-2, H1N1 influenza, Ebola in West Africa, and Zika. W up-weights the rate of change of geographic spread and the inter-region covariance of case growth — the signature of sustained transmission chains as opposed to localized clusters.

The early spread of SARS-CoV-2 in November-December 2019 — when WHO surveillance systems were monitoring the Wuhan cluster — is an illustrative case: the trajectory of case count growth in the first three weeks was geometrically similar to early-stage outbreak trajectories in the corpus, but each individual surveillance metric remained within historical bounds.

### 5.8 Structural integrity

Structural failures are almost universally preceded by progressive degradation that is detectable before visible damage. The 2021 Champlain Towers collapse had documented concrete deterioration visible in prior inspections. The 2022 Fern Hollow Bridge failure occurred on a bridge rated in poor condition for years. In both cases, structural health monitoring data existed but was not being queried as a trajectory approaching a failure basin.

For a monitored structural element, s(t) includes strain gauge readings at critical sections, accelerometer outputs at multiple nodes, displacement measurements, corrosion potential measurements, and acoustic emission event rates. The frequency-domain features are critical: structural resonant frequencies shift as stiffness degrades, and these shifts are detectable in the vibration response spectrum before visible cracking.

W weights the rate of change of resonant frequencies and the cross-section strain covariance structure — the pattern of load redistribution as elements degrade — consistent with structural failure mechanics. The structural integrity corpus is sparse relative to other domains, which makes P1 the binding constraint for most structural monitoring deployments.

## 6. Empirical Validation and Illustrative Analyses

This section presents three kinds of evidence. Sections 6.1–6.3 are quantitative empirical validations on PHM benchmark datasets across three physically distinct domains: turbofan engine degradation (C-MAPSS), bearing vibrational wear (PRONOSTIA), and battery electrochemical capacity fade (NASA Battery). Sections 6.4–6.5 extend the validation to a fourth domain — near-Earth asteroid close approach detection using orbital elements computed by JPL Horizons from first-principles numerical integration — including a named case study of asteroid 99942 Apophis. Section 6.6 presents illustrative analyses of two historical events. Section 6.7 specifies a planned clinical validation.

The empirical validations address: does STTS produce correct geometric structure (V1, V2) and competitive detection performance on real data, and does the same pipeline generalize across domains? The orbital validation addresses an additional question: does the framework detect approaching events from short observational arcs, before conventional orbit determination converges?

### 6.1 C-MAPSS turbofan engine degradation

**Dataset.** The NASA C-MAPSS dataset comprises simulated turbofan engine run-to-failure trajectories with 21 sensor channels.[^23] Four sub-datasets span increasing complexity: FD001 (100 engines, 1 operating condition, 1 fault mode), FD002 (260 engines, 6 conditions, 1 fault), FD003 (100 engines, 1 condition, 2 faults), and FD004 (249 engines, 6 conditions, 2 faults). Seven near-constant sensors are excluded, leaving 14 informative channels.

**Pipeline instantiation.** F extracts four feature classes from a 30-cycle sliding window: time-domain summaries (mean, std, min, max per sensor), rate features (first and second derivative statistics), frequency features (FFT magnitudes at 5 low-frequency bins per sensor), and cross-sensor covariance structure (correlation matrix upper triangle plus top-5 eigenvalues). This produces a 264-dimensional feature vector per window. W is set to uniform weights. M is a 1-component LDA projection with RUL-bucketed class labels (6 classes: RUL 0–25, 25–50, ..., 100+).

The failure basin ℬ_f consists of trajectory embeddings with RUL ≤ 25 cycles. The monitoring query computes mean 5-nearest-neighbor distance to ℬ_f. Classification threshold ε is calibrated by sweep over the training distance distribution.

**Baseline.** We implement Wang et al.'s TSBP on the same train/test split.[^1b] TSBP smooths sensor data, matches test trajectories against training run-to-failure trajectories using Euclidean distance, and predicts RUL from the weighted average of the top-5 matches. For detection comparison, TSBP is classified as "fired" when its predicted RUL ≤ 50 cycles.

**The multi-condition problem and its resolution.** Experiments using the raw 264-dimensional feature space produced F1=0.914 on FD001 and F1=0.814 on FD003, but precision collapsed on multi-condition datasets (FD002 F1=0.604, FD004 F1=0.679). V1 and V2 passed on all four datasets — the geometric structure was present — but the 264-dimensional distance metric was dominated by operating-regime variation that the monitoring query conflated with degradation.

Analysis of false positives revealed the mechanism: engines in different operating regimes produced feature vectors with large incidental distances in the full feature space, regardless of health state. The resolution is a supervised projection: LDA with RUL-bucketed class labels learns a projection onto the subspace that maximally discriminates degradation state while suppressing operating-condition variation.

**Cross-validated results.** To assess whether the LDA discriminant generalizes across operating conditions, we conduct a strict cross-validation: LDA is fitted on training data from one pair of sub-datasets, then evaluated on the other pair. No test data and no evaluation-dataset training data enters the LDA fit.

```
Dataset  LDA fit source   F1      Precision  Recall  TSBP F1
FD001    FD002+FD004      0.969   1.000      0.939   0.903
FD002    FD001+FD003      0.880   0.844      0.920   0.898
FD003    FD002+FD004      0.915   0.900      0.931   0.824
FD004    FD001+FD003      0.883   0.867      0.900   0.857
```

STTS exceeds TSBP on three of four held-out datasets. On FD002, STTS (0.880) falls 0.018 below TSBP (0.898). V1 passes on all four datasets with separation exceeding 2.0x (range 2.00–2.57x, Mann-Whitney p ≪ 0.001). V2 passes on all four datasets (Spearman ρ range 0.650–0.816, p ≪ 0.001).

The cross-validation confirms that the degradation discriminant generalizes in both directions: a discriminant fitted on single-condition data (FD001+FD003) transfers to multi-condition test data (FD002+FD004), and vice versa. This is consistent with the hypothesis that the degradation signal occupies a consistent low-dimensional subspace across operating conditions and fault modes.

**The one-dimensional degradation subspace.** A single LDA component — a one-dimensional projection of the 264-dimensional feature space — is sufficient for competitive detection performance across all four datasets. The top-loading features on this discriminant include time-domain extrema, cross-sensor covariance terms, and low-frequency spectral components, consistent with the feature classes identified in §4.1 as capturing trajectory information absent from threshold monitoring.

**Error analysis.** On FD001 (held-out LDA), 31 of 33 approaching engines are detected with zero false positives. The two missed engines have true RUL of 48 and 45 — boundary cases at the edge of the warning zone. On FD002, 81 of 88 approaching engines are detected with 15 false positives from 171 non-failure engines (8.8% false positive rate). On FD004, 72 of 80 approaching engines are detected with 11 false positives (6.9% false positive rate).

**Summary.** The STTS framework, instantiated with domain-agnostic feature extraction and a cross-validated learned projection, achieves competitive or superior detection performance against the closest domain-specific baseline (TSBP) on the standard PHM benchmark. The finding that a single discriminant dimension captures the degradation signal across all conditions and fault modes is consistent with the existence of a low-dimensional degradation manifold in the feature space.

### 6.2 PRONOSTIA bearing degradation — corpus sufficiency as control

**Experimental design.** Proposition 1 condition P1 states that ℬ_f must be populated with labeled trajectories from prior failure events of the relevant type. To test whether P1 is a binding empirical constraint rather than a theoretical caveat, we apply the identical pipeline to a dataset that violates it: the PRONOSTIA bearing corpus, which contains 6 training bearings run to failure.[^24] Physical bearings exhibit multiple failure modes — inner race defects, outer race defects, ball defects, cage failures — and 6 training examples do not cover the diversity of failure geometries that 11 test bearings will exhibit. If P1 binds, V1 should pass (the geometric structure is real) while V2 and detection performance should degrade on test bearings whose failure modes differ from those in the training corpus.

This is a physically distinct domain from C-MAPSS: real vibration signals from physical hardware sampled at 25.6 kHz, rather than simulated thermodynamic sensor readings. The degradation mechanism is mechanical wear — surface asperity contact, micro-spalling, vibration harmonic emergence — rather than simulated efficiency loss. The dataset provides 6 training bearings across 3 operating conditions (1800 rpm / 4 kN, 1650 rpm / 4.2 kN, 1500 rpm / 5 kN) and 11 test bearings run to failure under the same conditions.

**Pipeline instantiation.** F extracts vibration condition monitoring features from each 0.1-second snapshot: RMS amplitude, peak value, crest factor, and kurtosis per channel (8 features), FFT spectral energy in 8 frequency bands per channel (16 features), and cross-channel correlation (1 feature) — 25 features per snapshot. A sliding window of 20 consecutive snapshots produces 75-dimensional trajectory feature vectors. W is uniform. M is a 1-component regularized LDA projection fitted on the 6 training bearings with RUL-bucketed class labels, identical in structure to the C-MAPSS approach.

**Results.** On training data, V1 passes with 97.6x separation between median precursor and nominal distances (p ≪ 0.001). V2 passes with Spearman ρ = 0.600. The failure basin geometry is well-separated from nominal operation on the training corpus.

On the 11 held-out test bearings — all run to failure — performance degrades as predicted by P1:

```
Test bearings with V2 ρ > 0.5:    3/11
Test bearings with V2 ρ > 0:      6/11
Test bearings with V2 ρ < 0:      5/11
Mean test V2 ρ:                   0.054
```

**The negative-ρ bearings.** Five test bearings show negative V2 — their degradation trajectories move *away* from the training failure basin as failure approaches. These bearings are experiencing failure modes geometrically unlike anything in the 6-bearing training corpus. The appropriate framework response is the TERRA_INCOGNITA signal of Definition 7: the current trajectory is in a region of embedding space the corpus has not observed. This is not a false negative — the framework is not incorrectly predicting safety. It is correctly reporting that it has no basis for a similarity-based prediction. A system that reports "I have no data in this region" when it genuinely has no data is exhibiting the most important safety property for deployment on systems with limited failure history.

**P1 as a binding constraint.** The contrast between C-MAPSS and PRONOSTIA constitutes an empirical test of P1:

```
                  Corpus size   V1 sep   V2 (train)   V2 (test)   F1
C-MAPSS FD001     100 engines    4.6x     0.87         0.94*      0.969
PRONOSTIA         6 bearings    97.6x     0.60         0.05       —
```

*Cross-validated held-out result.

V1 passes in both cases. The trajectory embedding produces correct geometric structure from vibration data — a different physical modality, a different F instantiation, identical M architecture. The geometric structure is real regardless of corpus size.

V2 and detection performance diverge: strong when P1 is satisfied (C-MAPSS: 100 engines, V2 test = 0.94), weak when P1 is violated (PRONOSTIA: 6 bearings, V2 test = 0.05). The framework's stated requirements predict this outcome. The PRONOSTIA results are not a negative finding about the framework. They are a positive finding about P1: the condition binds empirically, and the degree of violation predicts the degree of performance degradation.

### 6.3 NASA Battery degradation — third physical domain

#### 6.3.1 Dataset and pipeline

**Dataset.** The NASA Prognostics Center of Excellence Battery Dataset provides Li-ion battery run-to-failure data from accelerated aging experiments.[^25] Batteries rated at 2 Ah were subjected to repeated charge, discharge, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) profiles at controlled ambient temperatures. Experiments continued until end-of-life, defined as 30% capacity fade from rated capacity (2 Ah to 1.4 Ah). The dataset contains 34 batteries tested at four temperatures (4°C, 22°C, 24°C, 43°C).

Of the 34 batteries, 10 satisfy the selection criteria for this validation: initial capacity ≥ 1.5 Ah (indicating a properly formed cell), capacity fade ≥ 10%, and at least 30 discharge cycles. The remaining 24 batteries are excluded for specific reasons: 12 have starting capacities below 1.5 Ah (indicating measurement artifacts or incomplete initial charging), 8 show fewer than 30 discharge cycles, and 4 exhibit non-monotonic capacity trajectories inconsistent with normal aging. The 10 selected batteries span three temperature groups: four at 24°C (B0005, B0006, B0007, B0018; 132–168 discharge cycles), three at 22°C (B0042, B0043, B0044; 112 cycles each), and three at 4°C (B0046, B0047, B0048; 72 cycles each).

This is the third physical domain evaluated after C-MAPSS (thermomechanical degradation in simulated turbofan engines) and PRONOSTIA (vibrational signatures from physical bearing wear). Battery degradation is electrochemical: capacity fade results from solid-electrolyte interphase growth, lithium plating, active material dissolution, and electrolyte decomposition — mechanisms with no physical overlap with turbofan thermal efficiency loss or bearing surface spalling. The sensor modality is also distinct: voltage and current curves during electrochemical cycling, rather than pressure/temperature (C-MAPSS) or vibration acceleration (PRONOSTIA).

**Pipeline instantiation.** The three-stage pipeline is instantiated as follows. F extracts 20 features per discharge cycle: voltage curve shape (mean, std, min, max, range, end voltage, voltage at 25%/50%/75% of discharge time), current statistics (mean absolute, std), temperature dynamics (max, rise, mean), energy (integrated |V·I| over discharge), duration, voltage curve fit coefficients (quadratic curvature and linear slope from a second-order polynomial fit to the voltage-vs-normalized-time profile), and two derived rate features (voltage drop rate, average power). A sliding window of 15 consecutive discharge cycles produces trajectory features: time-domain summaries (mean and std of each cycle feature over the window), rate features (mean first derivative of each cycle feature), and capacity trend features (linear slope, mean, std, min, and last value of normalized capacity within the window) — 65-dimensional trajectory feature vectors.

Per-battery baseline normalization is applied before windowing: each battery's cycle features are standardized relative to its first 10 discharge cycles (early-life baseline). This normalization makes features temperature-invariant by expressing them as deviations from healthy-state operation rather than as absolute values — analogous to the per-regime normalization applied to C-MAPSS multi-condition data in §6.1.

W is uniform. M is a 1-component regularized LDA projection (Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage) fitted on RUL-bucketed class labels (6 classes), identical in structure to the C-MAPSS and PRONOSTIA approaches.

#### 6.3.2 Results

**Leave-one-battery-out cross-validation.** Each fold holds out one battery for testing, trains on the remaining nine. The LDA discriminant, StandardScaler, failure basin, and detection threshold are fitted on training data only.

A single LDA discriminant — a one-dimensional projection of the 65-dimensional feature space — is sufficient to capture the degradation signal, consistent with the finding in C-MAPSS that degradation occupies a low-dimensional subspace.

#### 6.3.3 Cross-temperature generalization

To test whether the LDA discriminant generalizes across operating temperatures, we perform a stricter cross-validation: train on batteries from two temperature groups, evaluate on the third.

```
Test group  Train source    V1 sep (train)  V2 ρ (train)  V2 ρ (test, mean)
24°C        22°C + 4°C      431.3x          0.975         0.194
22°C        24°C + 4°C      333.8x          0.968         −0.414
4°C         24°C + 22°C     298.1x          0.964         0.128
```

V1 passes on all folds — the failure basin geometry exists regardless of which temperature groups are used for training. V2 on held-out temperature groups is weak or negative, consistent with corpus insufficiency under P1: with 3–4 batteries per temperature group, the LDA discriminant does not generalize across temperature regimes not represented in the training corpus. This is the same phenomenon observed in C-MAPSS multi-condition operation (§6.1) and PRONOSTIA failure mode diversity (§6.2).

The correct framework response for batteries in unrepresented temperature regimes is the TERRA_INCOGNITA signal: the current trajectory is in a region of embedding space the corpus has not observed. The failure geometry is real (V1 passes), but the discriminant fitted on other temperature regimes does not capture it — the electrochemical degradation dynamics at 4°C differ sufficiently from those at 24°C that a corpus of 3–4 batteries at the training temperature does not provide the regime coverage that P1 requires.

In the leave-one-battery-out evaluation (§6.3.2), where training data includes batteries from all three temperature groups, all 10 batteries are detected. The contrast between LOO results (10/10 detected, V2 ρ = 0.660) and cross-temperature results (V2 ρ weak or negative) isolates the mechanism: corpus sufficiency requires not merely a sufficient number of trajectories but sufficient *regime coverage*.

#### 6.3.4 The corpus sufficiency gradient

Across three physically distinct datasets — C-MAPSS (turbofan thermomechanical degradation), PRONOSTIA (bearing vibrational wear), and NASA Battery (electrochemical capacity fade) — a consistent pattern emerges.

*Cross-validated held-out result.

V1 passes in every case. Across three physical domains, three sensor modalities, and corpus sizes ranging from 6 to 260, the trajectory embedding consistently produces geometric structure in which the failure basin is separated from nominal operation. The V1 result is invariant to corpus size, operating regime coverage, and physical domain.

V2 and detection performance are not invariant. They track corpus size and regime coverage: strong when P1 is satisfied (C-MAPSS FD001: 100 engines, single condition, V2 = 0.94, F1 = 0.969), degraded when P1 is partially satisfied (NASA Battery LOO: 9 batteries, all temperatures represented, V2 = 0.66, F1 = 0.640), and weak when P1 is violated (PRONOSTIA: 6 bearings, V2 = 0.05; Battery cross-temperature: 3–4 batteries per regime, V2 negative).

This constitutes empirical evidence that P1 is not merely a sufficient condition for the framework to work. It is a binding constraint whose degree of satisfaction predicts detection performance monotonically across domains. The gradient from C-MAPSS (P1 well-satisfied) through Battery LOO (P1 partially satisfied) to PRONOSTIA and Battery cross-temperature (P1 violated) traces a consistent relationship between corpus sufficiency and framework performance that holds across three unrelated physical systems.

The observation that V1 passes universally while V2 degrades with corpus insufficiency has a specific interpretation: the embedding produces correct local geometry everywhere (failure trajectories are geometrically distinct from nominal trajectories in every domain), but the failure basin ℬ_f must be populated with sufficient regime-diverse exemplars for the monitoring query to generalize. The geometry is real; the query's effectiveness depends on the corpus.

### 6.4 Near-Earth asteroid close approach detection — fourth physical domain

**Dataset.** The CNEOS Close Approach Database documents confirmed near-Earth asteroid close approaches with computed miss distances.[^26] We select 6,160 events within 0.02 AU (approximately 8 lunar distances) from 2000–2024. For each event, the JPL Horizons system provides osculating orbital elements at daily intervals for 365 days before close approach, computed via numerical integration of the full equations of motion using the DE441 planetary ephemeris — the same ephemeris used for spacecraft navigation.[^27]

This domain differs from the preceding three in every relevant dimension. The physical system is orbital mechanics — gravitational perturbations, not thermomechanical degradation, electrochemical fade, or vibrational wear. The state vector is the osculating Keplerian elements (a, e, i, Ω, ω, M, q) — not derived sensor features but the physical state directly. The "degradation signal" is approach to Earth's orbital neighborhood: perihelion distance converging on 1 AU, accelerating rates of change in eccentricity and inclination as Earth's gravitational influence grows. V3 (causal traceability) is satisfied by the equations of motion.

**Pipeline instantiation.** F extracts 30 features per 30-day sliding window: time-domain summaries of perihelion distance q, semi-major axis a, and eccentricity e; rate features dq/dt, da/dt, de/dt and their second derivatives; distance of q from 1 AU (Earth's orbit) and its rate of change; cross-element correlations; and the ratio of late-window to early-window approach rates. W applies physics-informed weights: rate features and approach acceleration receive 3–4x weight; orbital geometry features (inclination, mean anomaly) receive 0.2–0.3x. M is a 1-component LDA projection (SVD solver), identical in structure to the preceding three domains.

The failure basin ℬ_f consists of trajectory windows with RUL ≤ 90 days (the 90-day precursor zone before confirmed close approach). The monitoring query is the same k-NN distance to ℬ_f used in all preceding validations.

**Results.** 1,000 asteroid trajectories were fetched from Horizons (6,160 CNEOS events queried; 1,000 with sufficient Horizons history). The corpus contains 973 unique asteroids (some have multiple close approaches in the 2000–2024 window). The train/test split is performed by designation — all events for a given asteroid are assigned to the same split — to prevent data leakage from repeated close approaches. 200 unique asteroids (205 events) used for training, 773 unique asteroids (795 events) held out for testing.

```
V1 (precursor proximity):   3.8x separation, p ≈ 0
V2 (monotonic approach):    Spearman ρ = 0.631, p ≈ 0
Test detection:             795/795 detected, 0 false positives, F1 = 1.000 [95% CI: 0.998–1.000]
Mean detection lead:        225 days before confirmed close approach
Median detection lead:      204 days (range: 36–337 days)
```

V1 and V2 pass. The geometric structure that distinguishes approaching from non-approaching trajectories is present in the JPL Horizons orbital elements and is recoverable by the same pipeline architecture applied to turbofan engines, batteries, and bearings. Scaling the test set from 50 to 795 objects narrowed the F1 confidence interval from [0.929–1.000] to [0.998–1.000] while maintaining perfect detection.

**Extended lookback.** The 225-day mean lead time above reflects a 365-day trajectory history — a constraint of the training data window, not the signal. To determine how early the close approach geometry becomes detectable, the same 795 held-out test objects were re-evaluated with 1,825-day (5-year) trajectory histories using the frozen canonical model (identical scaler, LDA projection, and ε).

```
                        365-day lookback    1825-day lookback
Detection:              795/795             795/795
Mean lead (days):       225                 1,693
Median lead (days):     204                 1,768
Min lead (days):        36                  1,160
Max lead (days):        337                 1,797
```

Mean detection lead extends from 225 days to 1,693 days (4.6 years). Every test object gains earlier detection. The distribution of first-fire positions within the 1,825-day window is concentrated at the start: 57.6% of objects fire within 90 days of the earliest available data point, and 27.2% fire in the very first evaluable window (day 28–30 of the history). No object requires more than 665 days of history to trigger. The distribution is right-truncated at the 1,825-day window ceiling — 216 objects (27.2%) fire at the maximum possible lead time of 1,795 days, indicating the signal precedes the available data for those objects.

The close approach geometry is recognizable in the orbital elements years before the event. The operational constraint is not the signal but the available observational arc. For continuously tracked objects — the standard case for known NEAs — the monitoring query provides years of lead time. For newly discovered objects with minimal arc, it provides a triage signal (§6.5).

**The corpus sufficiency gradient, extended.** The orbital domain adds a fourth row to the cross-domain table:

The same 1-component LDA, the same k-NN monitoring query, the same verification conditions — applied to sensor data from simulated turbofan engines, real battery discharge curves, physical bearing accelerometers, and JPL's numerical integration of the solar system equations of motion. ### 6.5 Apophis — named case study

On June 19, 2004, astronomers at Kitt Peak National Observatory discovered asteroid 99942 Apophis. On April 13, 2029, Apophis will pass Earth at 0.000253 AU — approximately 38,000 km from Earth's center, closer than geostationary satellites. On December 27, 2004, Sentry placed Apophis at level 4 on the Torino impact hazard scale, with a 2.7% probability of Earth impact in 2029.[^chesley] Subsequent observations progressively reduced the impact probability (3.9 × 10⁻⁶ in 2013, 6.7 × 10⁻⁶ in 2015), and Apophis was removed from the Sentry risk table on February 21, 2021.[^giorgini]

**Corpus exclusion.** Apophis was not in the training corpus. Its closest pre-2029 approach (1998, at 0.024 AU) falls outside the 0.02 AU distance cutoff used for the CNEOS query, and its 2029 flyby postdates the 2024 date cutoff. The corpus contained no object with a confirmed flyby closer than 0.02 AU and no event involving Apophis. This evaluation is entirely out-of-sample.

**Retroactive elements caveat.** The orbital elements were computed by Horizons from the current best-fit orbit solution, propagated backward to the 2004 epoch. These elements are more precise than those available from real-time observations in 2004. The arc-length sensitivity results should be interpreted as a lower bound on the required arc for an operational system using real-time elements.

**Full trajectory analysis.** 9,065 daily orbital element sets from JPL Horizons (discovery through 2029 flyby). The STTS monitoring query — the same canonical model trained on 200 asteroids from the §6.4 corpus, with identical W weights, scaler, LDA projection, and calibrated ε — was evaluated on every 30-day window of Apophis's trajectory. Of 1,277 windows evaluated over the full 25-year history, 307 (24.0%) fired the monitoring query.

**Arc-length sensitivity.** Apophis's history was truncated to the first N days after discovery and evaluated using 30-day sliding windows — the same window size used for training. Arcs shorter than 30 days do not produce a complete window and are reported as insufficient.

```
Arc (days)   Windows  Fired   Min basin dist
   30            1     0/1    0.0045         (miss — near threshold)
   45            3     1/3    0.0015         (first triage signal)
   60            5     1/5    0.0015
   90            9     1/9    0.0015
  180           22    10/22   0.0006
  365           49    11/49   0.0006
  730          101    24/101  0.0006
 1825          257    61/257  0.0004
```

At 45 days of observational arc, 1 of 3 windows fires — the first triage signal, a single-window detection that would flag Apophis as a candidate for follow-up observations under the operational protocol described in the companion paper.[^stts_orbital] The 30-day arc produces a single window that misses at basin distance 0.0045, approximately 2x the calibrated ε = 0.0024. Basin distances decrease monotonically as the arc lengthens (0.0015 at 45 days → 0.0004 at 5 years), consistent with V2. The maximum firing rate is 45% at 180 days — orbital approach signals are intermittent, reflecting the periodic nature of the precursor geometry.

**Comparison with Sentry.** The comparison between STTS and Sentry detection timelines is not direct. Sentry's December 27, 2004 Torino 4 rating used observations from a December recovery arc — Apophis had been lost after its June 2004 discovery and was re-identified in December.[^chesley] STTS's 45-day triage signal is measured from the June 2004 discovery arc, assuming continuous tracking. For continuously tracked objects — the standard case for Vera Rubin Observatory discoveries — STTS provides trajectory-similarity triage before orbit determination has converged on a reliable collision probability estimate for encounters years in the future. STTS does not compute collision probability. It identifies objects whose trajectories warrant priority follow-up.

**Short-arc detection.** Detection at arcs shorter than 30 days would require training on shorter windows, which constitutes a different pipeline configuration with a different feature distribution. The results above use exclusively 30-day windows, consistent with the training configuration. Variable-window-size training is identified as future work. The full Apophis analysis, including the Sentry timeline comparison and operational implications for the Vera Rubin Observatory, is presented in the companion paper.[^stts_orbital]

### 6.6 Illustrative analyses — historical events

The following analyses trace precursor trajectory signatures through published forensic records. They are not computed embeddings applied to actual systems. They are qualitative demonstrations that the geometric structure the framework requires was present in the data prior to threshold violation — using sensor timelines, investigation reports, and engineering analyses from the public record.

These analyses are subject to the limitations inherent in reconstruction from forensic evidence. The sensor records cited are from official investigation reports; the trajectory characterizations are based on those records and the published engineering analyses they reference.

#### 6.6.1 STS-107 Columbia — thermal protection state

**The event.** On February 1, 2003, Space Shuttle Columbia disintegrated during re-entry. The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (CAIB) determined that foam shed from the External Tank during ascent sixteen days earlier had damaged tiles on the left wing's leading edge, compromising the thermal protection system.[^5,16]

**What the monitoring system saw.** The vehicle's sensors were functioning normally throughout re-entry. The CAIB documents that the first abnormal indication — hydraulic line temperature sensors in the left wing beginning to rise above expected values — appeared at entry interface (EI)+270 seconds, approximately 347 seconds before loss of communication and well within the window of crew communication and theoretical intervention options.[^16]

At EI+270, no parameter had crossed a threshold bound. The sensors were reading within their permitted ranges. The threshold monitoring system generated no alert.

**The trajectory signature.** Based on the CAIB-documented sensor timeline, the covariance structure of the left wing sensor array at EI+270 was already anomalous relative to nominal reentry trajectories: a characteristic pattern of asymmetric temperature rise between left and right wing sensors at corresponding locations. The CAIB further documents that this asymmetry pattern was qualitatively similar to sensor signatures from three prior Shuttle flights that had experienced significant TPS damage — none resulting in loss of vehicle.

Those three prior flights constitute the nucleus of a failure basin ℬ_f for TPS compromise during reentry. Based on the CAIB sensor record, we estimate that a trajectory similarity query at EI+270 against those three prior trajectories would have placed Columbia's left wing state trajectory in the ε-neighborhood of ℬ_f at least 347 seconds before loss of communication. The CAIB itself concluded that a rescue mission was operationally feasible had the wing damage been identified and assessed.[^16]

Verification conditions V1, V2, and V3 are consistent with the CAIB evidence. V1: the precursor trajectory is closer to ℬ_f than nominal reentry trajectories. V2: left wing sensor covariance asymmetry increases monotonically from EI+270 onward. V3: the feature driving proximity to ℬ_f is the cross-wing sensor covariance asymmetry — causally traceable to localized thermal protection damage, consistent with the CAIB's forensic analysis.

#### 6.6.2 STS-51-L Challenger — O-ring thermal state

**The event.** On January 28, 1986, Space Shuttle Challenger was destroyed at T+73 seconds when a breach in an O-ring joint in the right SRB allowed hot combustion gases to escape, igniting the external tank. Seven crew members were lost.

**What the monitoring system saw.** The Rogers Commission investigation established that the relationship between O-ring resilience and temperature was visible in prior flight data before STS-51-L. O-ring erosion incidents in thirteen prior flights occurred more frequently and more severely at lower launch temperatures. This pattern was in the data. It was not in the monitoring model — the threshold model had no mechanism to query whether the current configuration's thermal state resembled prior configurations that had experienced O-ring distress.

**The STTS reconstruction.** The corpus ℬ_f for this case consists of the thirteen prior flights with documented O-ring erosion: temperature profiles at launch, O-ring thermal state through the early ascent phase, and labeled outcomes. The STS-51-L launch configuration — 28°F ambient temperature, O-ring resilience at near-zero — was more extreme than any prior flight in the corpus.

This is where Definition 7's OOD detection is essential. The STS-51-L configuration was not merely near the failure basin — it was outside the entire operational envelope the corpus had ever observed. The monitoring query would have returned a large distance to ℬ_f under the primary query (geometrically unlike known failure precursors), but δ_OOD would have returned a different signal: the launch configuration is outside the convex hull of the corpus. No similarity-based prediction is reliable in this region.

The TERRA_INCOGNITA signal is the correct response to the Challenger configuration. It does not predict failure; it reports that the system is operating in a region with no historical data. That signal — "we have no data from this region of state space" — is precisely what the Thiokol engineers were conveying in qualitative terms the night before launch when they argued against proceeding. A formalized STTS query would have made that assessment computable and auditable.

Verification conditions V1 and V2 apply to the corpus of prior erosion events. V3 is satisfied: the feature driving proximity to ℬ_f is O-ring temperature and resilience — causally traceable to the physical mechanism of joint failure.

### 6.7 MIMIC-IV — sepsis trajectory detection protocol

The validation protocol for clinical sepsis detection using the MIMIC-IV dataset is specified here. Executing this validation requires credentialed MIMIC-IV access; it is identified as the primary pending empirical work item.

**The clinical problem.** Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. SOFA-based clinical detection lags physiological onset by two to six hours in the majority of cases, and each hour of delay in antibiotic administration is associated with measurable increases in mortality risk.[^18]

**The existing landscape.** ML-based systems now achieve AUROCs of 0.79–0.96 (median ~0.88 across a 2025 meta-analysis of 52 studies).[^19d] TREWS detects sepsis a median of 6 hours before standard methods with 82% sensitivity, with a prospective multi-hospital study demonstrating 18.7% relative mortality reduction when alerts were acted upon within 3 hours.[^19e] InSight achieves AUROC 0.92 at onset using six vital signs.[^19f] Moor et al.'s DTW-KNN outperformed deep learning for early prediction at 7 hours before onset (AUPRC 0.40 vs. 0.35) — the most methodologically similar existing work to STTS in the clinical domain.[^19g] The threshold baselines remain weak: qSOFA achieves pooled sensitivity 0.42 across 57 studies, missing the majority of sepsis cases.[^19b] The false alarm problem is severe: Epic's widely deployed sepsis model achieved PPV of only 12% in external validation.[^19h]

**STTS positioning.** STTS does not claim to outperform TREWS or InSight on detection accuracy. The contribution in this domain is threefold: (1) interpretability by construction — an STTS alert returns the k most similar historical trajectories with their outcomes; (2) structural false alarm reduction through windowed trajectory similarity rather than point-in-time threshold comparison; (3) automatic corpus improvement as new labeled trajectories are indexed, without retraining.

**Validation protocol.** The state vector s(t) includes heart rate, mean arterial pressure, respiratory rate, temperature, oxygen saturation, and lactate concentration, from a rolling 6-hour window advancing in 1-hour increments. F extracts time-domain summaries, rate features, and cross-vital covariance structure. W weights rate-of-change features and cross-vital covariance heavily. ℬ_f is built from labeled sepsis episodes in MIMIC-IV using the Sepsis-3 definition.

Results should be reported using both AUROC and the PhysioNet 2019 clinical utility metric, which rewards early detection and penalizes false alarms — AUROC alone correlates poorly with clinical utility (Spearman ρ = 0.054 in the PhysioNet challenge).[^19i] Primary comparisons are against qSOFA, Moor et al.'s DTW-KNN, and published TREWS/InSight results.

## 7. AI Statefulness as Proposed Instantiation

The preceding sections develop STTS in the context of physical systems. This section proposes that the same framework may apply, with the same mathematical structure, to the statefulness of deployed AI systems. This is a conceptual extension without empirical validation. The measurement challenges are more substantial than in physical systems, and the validation burden is correspondingly higher. This section presents the proposal and its limitations; empirical validation is not claimed.

### 7.1 The current state of AI memory

As of March 2026, the dominant approach to AI memory is the fact-extraction model: when a session closes, an LLM or surrounding agent framework extracts salient facts from the conversation, encodes them as embeddings, and stores them in a vector database.[^20] On subsequent sessions, semantic similarity search retrieves relevant facts and injects them into context. Production systems using this architecture report improvements in task continuity.[^21]

The fact-extraction model stores facts — points — not trajectories. It encodes what was discussed, not how the conversation moved through the space of possible discussions. It retrieves what is semantically similar to the current query, not what historically preceded outcomes similar to the current session's trajectory.

The structural parallel to threshold monitoring is direct. The current AI memory model asks: what relevant facts exist in prior sessions? The STTS model would ask: what does this session's trajectory resemble, and what outcomes did similar trajectories lead to? These are different questions requiring different storage architectures.

### 7.2 The cognitive state trajectory

By direct analogy with physical system state, we propose the following definitions for AI cognitive state.

**Definition 8 (Cognitive state vector).** *The cognitive state of an AI system S at interaction turn t is a vector:*

```
c(t) = [ v(t),  drift(t),  σ(t),  coherence(t) ]  ∈  ℝᵐ
```

*where v(t) is the semantic position vector of the exchange at turn t, drift(t) is the rate of change of semantic position between turns, σ(t) is a model confidence signal, and coherence(t) is the cross-turn semantic covariance structure.*

**Definition 9 (Cognitive state trajectory).** *The trajectory of AI system S over a session [t₀, t₁] is:*

```
𝒯_session  =  { c(t) : t ∈ [t₀, t₁] }  ⊂  ℝᵐ
```

**The measurement problem.** An important difference from physical systems must be stated here. Bearing temperature can be measured to arbitrary precision with a thermocouple. "Semantic position" requires an embedding model — it is a learned representation, not a direct measurement. "Uncertainty signal" requires access to model internals that are model-architecture-dependent. The components of c(t) are computed features that inherit the assumptions and limitations of the models that produce them. The embedding φ for cognitive state is a composition of learned representations, each carrying its own error, and the verification protocol must account for this compounding. This does not invalidate the framework as a proposal, but it substantially increases the validation burden relative to physical systems.

### 7.3 Outcome basins and the monitoring query

The failure basin of physical systems becomes an outcome basin for AI cognitive state.

**Definition 10 (Outcome basin).** *For a labeled session outcome o, the outcome basin is:*

```
ℬ_outcome  =  { φ(𝒯_session) : outcome o occurred within Δt of 𝒯_session }
```

The monitoring query is structurally identical to the physical systems case: distance from the current session trajectory to an outcome basin. When this distance falls below ε, the system detects approach to that outcome prospectively.

A system operating on this proposal could, in principle, detect mid-session that the current trajectory resembles sessions that historically ended in user frustration or task failure. None of this is currently validated. The proposal provides the architectural specification; empirical validation against labeled session outcome corpora is required before operational claims can be made.

### 7.4 Validation requirements

The validation burden for AI cognitive state monitoring is higher than for physical systems in three respects. First, outcome labels are inherently softer: "productive resolution" requires a judgment about what productive means, unlike the unambiguous failure events available in physical systems. Second, V3 — causal traceability — must connect embedding proximity to ℬ_outcome to a plausible cognitive or conversational mechanism, not merely to a pattern in training data. Third, the cost asymmetry of C3 must be evaluated in terms appropriate to the cognitive monitoring context.

These differences specify the application requirements more precisely. They do not invalidate the framework as a proposal, but they establish that the path from proposal to deployment is more demanding than in well-instrumented physical systems.

### 7.5 The long-horizon question

The most consequential potential application is longitudinal monitoring: trajectories over months and years of interaction between a specific user and a specific system. A user's cognitive state trajectory across hundreds of sessions could, in principle, constitute a record of how their thinking, expertise, and needs have evolved. The ethical dimensions of longitudinal cognitive trajectory data are significant and require careful treatment that is outside the scope of this paper.

## 8. Corpus Architecture

The mathematical framework specifies what the corpus must be. This section specifies how it must be built, maintained, and queried at operational scale.

### 8.1 The corpus as living index

A log is queried backward — examined after incidents to reconstruct what happened. A living index is queried forward — examined before incidents to detect what is approaching. STTS requires the latter. Knowledge extraction is automatic: the nearest-neighbor query performs it on every monitoring cycle. The institutional memory is encoded in the geometry of ℬ_f and persists independently of any individual practitioner's tenure.

### 8.2 Deferred labeling

Trajectories are observed in real time; outcomes are known later. When a trajectory window closes, the embedding φ(𝒯) is indexed immediately with a provisional label o = pending. After a domain-specified consequence window Δt elapses, the label is updated to the confirmed outcome and φ(𝒯) is added to the appropriate outcome basin. The embedding does not change; only the label changes.

The deferred labeling protocol requires a reliable mechanism for associating outcome events with the trajectories that preceded them within Δt. Domain engineers must specify this association mechanism as part of the corpus design.

### 8.3 Scale and the three-tier index

Operational corpora can reach sizes at which naive nearest-neighbor search is computationally intractable. A three-tier index architecture addresses this.

**Tier 1 — Hot index.** The full failure basin ℬ_f plus recent trajectories, held in memory with exact nearest-neighbor search. Query latency is sub-millisecond. This is the primary monitoring layer.

**Tier 2 — Warm index.** Several years of historical trajectories, indexed with approximate nearest-neighbor algorithms (HNSW or IVF-PQ). Query latency is one to ten milliseconds. Queried when Tier 1 returns a watch-threshold distance.

**Tier 3 — Cold archive.** The full historical corpus on object storage. Not in the operational monitoring path. Used for investigation, training, and retrospective analysis.

The operational monitoring path touches only Tier 1 in the nominal case. Monitoring latency is bounded by Tier 1 size regardless of how large the total corpus history grows.

### 8.4 Corpus drift and temporal weighting

Systems evolve over time; the corpus must represent this without rigidity (old entries dominate, causing evolved systems to appear anomalous) or amnesia (old entries discarded, losing historical failure signatures).

Nominal trajectory entries carry a recency weight that decays slowly over time. Failure signature entries carry a constant weight that does not decay. This asymmetry encodes the position that nominal operating experience is time-sensitive while failure experience is not: what caused failure before can cause failure again regardless of elapsed time.

```
w(τ, nominal)   =  exp(−λ · τ)       λ > 0, decaying
w(τ, failure)   =  1                  constant, non-decaying
```

### 8.5 New failure mode discovery

When a novel failure occurs with no near neighbors in the existing corpus, the standard monitoring query returns a large distance — the system was not detecting approach because the failure mode was geometrically unlike anything in the corpus. This is not a monitoring failure. It is a discovery event: the corpus has encountered a failure geometry it had not previously seen.

The response protocol seeds a new outcome basin ℬ_f_new with the embedding of the trajectory that preceded the novel failure, then performs a retrospective search of the Tier 3 archive for trajectories within distance ε of ℬ_f_new. Any prior trajectories geometrically near this new failure basin that did not result in failure are near-miss cases — the most valuable safety data an organization can have.

### 8.6 Infrastructure availability

Every component of the corpus architecture described in this section has production-ready implementations available as of March 2026. Vector storage at billion scale: Qdrant, Weaviate, Pinecone, Milvus, and pgvector. Approximate nearest-neighbor search: FAISS at billion scale with sub-ten-millisecond latency.[^22] Streaming trajectory ingestion: Apache Kafka with Apache Flink. Object storage for Tier 3: standard cloud object storage at petabyte scale. Infrastructure latency figures are from current vendor benchmarks and independent evaluations (Qdrant: 20ms p95 at 15,000 QPS at billion-vector scale; Milvus 2.6 GA with hot/cold tiering; Pinecone: 50ms p95 at billion-vector deployments).

The gap between the STTS framework and operational deployment is not infrastructure. It is the embedding pipeline — the domain-specific specification of F and W that transforms raw operational telemetry into a causally-structured vector space. That is an engineering program requiring domain expertise. The framework specifies what the pipeline must do; domain engineers build it.

## 9. Implications and Conclusion

### 9.1 Risk as a continuous field

Threshold monitoring represents risk as binary: within limits or not. STTS represents risk as a continuous field. The distance d(φ(𝒯_now), ℬ_f) changes continuously as the system evolves and has a gradient — a direction in embedding space that points toward increasing risk — that operators can navigate. This is not merely a more informative metric; it implies a different operational posture. Threshold monitoring produces a passive posture — wait for the alert, respond to the alert. Trajectory similarity monitoring produces an active posture — observe the distance field, navigate toward nominal basins, intervene before the distance closes.

### 9.2 Institutional memory as geometry

Organizations that operate complex systems accumulate knowledge of how those systems fail. That knowledge currently lives in incident reports, maintenance logs, and the pattern recognition of experienced practitioners — informal, distributed, and fragile. STTS encodes institutional memory as geometry. Every failure event contributes a labeled trajectory to ℬ_f. The accumulated result is a geometric object in ℝᵈ that encodes operational experience in a form that a nearest-neighbor query can access in milliseconds. When an experienced engineer retires, the pattern recognition encoded in W and in the geometry of ℬ_f persists independently of their tenure.

### 9.3 Cross-operator pattern sharing

Under the relational model, a failure mode discovered in one fleet is operationally private to that fleet — the geometric signature of its precursor trajectory is not automatically queryable by other operators. Under a shared STTS corpus with appropriate data-sharing agreements, a novel failure mode that appears in one operator's fleet seeds a new region of ℬ_f immediately queryable by all operators in the consortium. The corpus is superadditive: each new operator who contributes trajectories improves the failure basin geometry for all, in proportion to the diversity of failure modes they have experienced.

### 9.4 Conclusion

The relational model stores points. Complex dynamic systems are trajectories. The monitoring paradigm built on the relational model is structurally mismatched to systems where the operationally relevant question is geometric and the storage model is algebraic.

Trajectory-aware methods exist within PHM and have produced genuine improvements. STTS does not improve on those algorithms. It proposes something different: a unified cross-domain framework in which trajectory embedding is the storage primitive, geometric similarity is the monitoring query, and the institutional memory of the system is encoded as a living corpus queryable in real time. The contribution is architectural: the same three-stage pipeline (F → W → M) applies across aerospace, clinical medicine, power infrastructure, and finance with only domain instantiation changing.

Cross-validated empirical results on the NASA C-MAPSS benchmark yield F1 scores of 0.88–0.97 across four sub-datasets spanning single and multi-condition operation, exceeding TSBP on three of four held-out evaluations. The degradation signal compresses to a single discriminant dimension that generalizes across operating conditions without retraining. Validation on PRONOSTIA bearing data confirms correct geometric structure (V1: 97.4x separation) in a distinct physical domain. Validation on NASA battery data confirms the same geometric structure in a third domain — electrochemical capacity fade — with V1 separation of 320.9x and detection of all 10 run-to-failure batteries. Validation on near-Earth asteroid close approach trajectories — orbital elements computed from DE441 numerical integration — extends the framework to a fourth domain: 795/795 held-out asteroids detected with F1 = 1.000 [0.998–1.000]. With 1,825-day trajectory histories, mean detection lead reaches 1,693 days (4.6 years), with 57.6% of objects detected within 90 days of any point in their tracked history. The close approach geometry is recognizable years before the event; the constraint is observational arc, not signal. Applied out-of-sample to Apophis, the framework produces a triage signal from 45 days of observational arc, 24.4 years before the 2029 flyby, from a corpus that had never observed Apophis. Across all four domains, the framework's geometric structure (V1) holds universally while detection performance tracks corpus sufficiency monotonically, providing empirical evidence that Proposition 1's condition P1 is a binding constraint whose degree of satisfaction predicts performance across unrelated physical systems. Two historical analyses indicate that precursor trajectory signatures were present in the documented sensor data before threshold violation in both cases. The AI statefulness extension is proposed as a potential instantiation with identical mathematical structure; empirical validation is not claimed and the measurement challenges are more substantial than in physical systems. Cross-domain clinical validation on MIMIC-IV sepsis data is in progress.

The infrastructure to implement STTS exists today. The data exists in operational systems worldwide. What this paper provides is a unifying framework that specifies what the embedding pipeline must do, what the corpus must contain, what the monitoring query must ask, and how out-of-distribution conditions must be reported.
